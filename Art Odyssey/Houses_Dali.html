Welcome to Dalí's House

Salvador Felipe Jacinto Dalí Domènech was born May 11, 1904, in the Catalonian town of Figueres in northeastern Spain. Known for his theatrical behavior and appearance (especially his cartoonishly waxed mustache) and for paintings (frenzied swirls of delirious, if not demented, subject matter) and found-object sculptures (e.g., Lobster Telephone, 1938, a handset sheathed in a crustacean carapace), Dalí became synonymous with Surrealism. He made paintings, sculptures and films about the dreams he had. He painted melting clocks and floating eyes, clouds that look like faces and rocks that look like bodies. At 25, he met his future wife and lifelong companion, Gala, at his family’s vacation home in Cadaqués.

― HISTORY

Rembrandt’s House Museum, the residence of the Dutch painter Rembrandt Harmenszoon van Rijn (1606 – 1669) from 1639 to 1659, showcases an extensive collection of etchings by the Master, paintings by artists who inspired him, and numerous personal belongings of the artist. The building, erected in 1606, the same year Rembrandt was born in Leiden, underwent remodeling by architect Jacob van Campen, also known for designing the Royal Palace on Dam Square, a few years before Rembrandt’s occupancy. During the 19 years Rembrandt lived there, he created some of his most celebrated works, including the Night Watch, which is displayed at the Rijksmuseum.This period marked a time of great prosperity for Rembrandt as both an artist and a teacher, with his studio generating a vast array of artworks. Over his lifetime, Rembrandt produced approximately 300 paintings, 290 etchings, and 2,000 drawings, with the Rembrandt House holding an almost complete collection of his etchings.

However, Rembrandt’s fortunes eventually waned, attributed to his lavish lifestyle and a diminishing art market. In 1658, after continual difficulties in settling his debts, he was declared bankrupt and forced to relocate to a more modest rented accommodation on Rozengracht. For the subsequent 250 years, the Rembrandt house was inhabited by various tenants until it was acquired by the city council and entrusted to a foundation. The building underwent renovations from 1908 to 1911 before being inaugurated as a museum devoted to the artist's legacy.

― MODERN DAY

Considered a National Treasure by Dutch government, contemporary new wing was inaugurated in 1998, adjacent to the main building. 2022-23 saw a significant upgrade, expanding its exhibition space by 30%. The house's layout mirrors the typical design of its era, with a restoration that remarkably adheres to the original architecture.

Modern visitors will find particular interest in exploring Rembrandt’s bedroom, which features a quaintly small bed; his expansive studio, where numerous famed artworks were created; and his cabinet, home to an array of natural and crafted objects that influenced Rembrandt’s pieces.The era's furniture and artifacts are meticulously arranged alongside prints, sculptures, and a selection of paintings by Rembrandt’s contemporaries. The museum also exhibits nearly all of Rembrandt’s prints and illustrates the graphic techniques he employed.

Visitors can also explore his reception room, where he conducted sales of his paintings; the kitchen, which includes the bedstee for the maid, the main studio and the studio used by his students. Additionally, a substantial storeroom holds various specimens collected by Rembrandt, along with a functioning press used for printing etchings.
